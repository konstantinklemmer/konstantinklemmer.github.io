The Culture of Silence surrounding Rape: Evidence from New York

Yu Chen, Matt Dwyer, Davey Ives, Konstantin Klemmer, Rachel Lim, Sunglyoung Kim

June 12, 2017

Introduction

In 2015, the New York City Police Department released its incident reports for felonies, following a greater citywide trend of data transparency. The data released details the location, time, date, and day of occurrence, time and date of reported, sector, precinct, borough, jurisdiction and type of felony that reported in 2015 across the city. The seven felonies in the dataset correspond with the FBI’s seven major felonies, which cover murder, rape, robbery, assault, burglary, grand larceny, and grand larceny of motor vehicles. Through the release of NYPD’s crime data, this study aims to investigate the spatial and temporal spread of crimes across NYC, along with the difference between crime occurrence and when the crime was reported. By employing statistical and mapping methods, the data can answer high level questions about what time of day or day of the week see increased criminal activity, how specific types of felonies cluster across the city, and how crime reporting times vary between felonies.

Data and Methods

The data provided by the NYPD reflected simple digital coordinates. For the initial data exploration, location granularity was narrowed to the census tract level instead of zip code level to improve the level of accuracy. This level of accuracy allows location specific characteristics that may be left out on the zip code level to be identified. Using this level of location granularity, the spatial spread of crime counts across the city for each respective felony can be computed. Out of these 7 felonies, the number of reported assault and robbery incidents are mapped out by census tract. In Figure 2, the highest number of assaults is observed on Rikers Island, which houses New York City’s main jail complex.

Figure 3 shows the number of robberies per census tract, with areas such as Flatbush and Harlem observing high numbers of robberies.

The median time gap is computed with high values fixed to the 95% quantile to reduce the effect of heavy outliers in the data, providing a more accurate representation of the difference. The results are useful in revealing the key felonies contributing to the long time gap. For instance, Rape has the highest mean time gap of 63 days, compared to robbery which has the lowest mean time gap of 2 days. This reveals that rape victims are more likely to delay reporting the incident compared to a victim of robbery. However, these mean values may be influenced by outliers that could have skewed the data. Hence, the median time gap and standard deviation is computed to reflect the actual distribution more accurately, reducing the likelihood of generalising the data.

Discussion

Reporting Time Delays

One of our key findings what that the largest differences in reporting time occurred between the seven felony types. Below are possible explanations for why these differences occur.

  • Murder: Murders had both short and long reporting times. This is supported by the second highest standard deviation amount. The range in reporting time may be due to the fact that it requires a second party to report such a crime. Shorter reporting times could be linked to incidents where a body is left in the public realm, or the murder attracted attention of other bystanders, such as the sound of a loud gunshot. Longer reporting times in the murder category are most likely due to incidents where a person has gone missing for some time, yet was not found and/or ruled a murder until a later date.

  • Rape: Rape had the longest mean and median reporting times. This is due to a variety of factors that will be discussed more in depth.

  • Robbery: Robbery had a shorter mean reporting time. This was possibly because it directly impacts a person and they have an incentive to report it to the police. Additionally, there is little social stigma with respect to talking about being robbed.

  • Felony Assault: Assault also had a shorter mean reporting time. This is likely because medical trauma occurred and a detailed report was required/assisted by medical professionals.

  • Burglary: Burglary has a comparatively longer reporting time. This could be due to the fact that the incident occurs when the victim is not present. This is an extra step in reporting whereby the victim needs to assess the incident, then report what is missing.

  • Grand Larceny: Grand Larceny had one of the longest reporting time and the most number of events. This could be attributed to a number of factors, such as the wide range of items covered under this offense. Also it’s victims are both residential and commercial property owners. The latter for example would generally have Grand Larceny Motor Vehicle - Similar to Burglary, Grand Larceny Motor Vehicle had a longer reporting time due to the extra step needed to confirm that a crime had taken place.

Rape

1,080 reported incidents of rape occurred during 2015. However large discrepancies between when the incident occurred and when the incident was reported were found in the dataset. The difference between the two dates reveals an interesting trend. While rape crime accounts for only 1.41 percent of total crime, the median reporting date is 2 days, with a standard deviation is 107.03. This is particularly interesting because in comparison to the figures for other felonies, rape stands out with a distinctively higher time gap. To better visualise this data, the number of days required to report rape across census tracts is presented through a histogram. Figure 6 shows that 50 percent of rape crime is reported in three days but 30 percent of the crime is reported after 30 days or later.

Figure 6: Reporting times for rape

This draws our attention to the reasons for delaying reporting a crime following the incident. This is contingent on a number of factors. Existing research have looked into why victims don’t report rape crimes to the authorities. Courtney Aherns discusses this in her paper “Being Silenced: The Impact of Negative Social Reactions on the Disclosure of Rape.”. This is supported by Kilpatrick, Dean G., et al. in “Criminal Victimization: Lifetime Prevalence, Reporting to Police, and Psychological Impact.”. However, there remains a gap in the research in understanding more in depth reasons for delaying reporting a crime. This includes the need for a better understanding of the psychological and physical effects of rape on the victim, to better explain the 30 days after the crime.

Connecting the spatial and temporal perspective, we can identify areas that show both, a high number of rapes as well as a high mean reporting time. While spatial coordinates for rape are only reported on a precinct level, this still allows us to identify especially bad precincts (see Figure 7). Our findings may help in planning and coordinating policy interventions to tackle rape. Furthermore, we hope to raise awareness for an often surpressed issue by showing where rape is especially prevalent and where reporting times are especially long.

Limitations and Further Research

While the report has successfully revealed the long time gap between occurrence date and reporting date for specific felonies such as rape, more research needs to be conducted to better understand the reasons behind this time lag. The report has proposed some general reasons, but effective response to the problem will require more specific information on the age groups of victims, and background knowledge of the perpetrator.

The research on rape incidents in the city is limited by the nature of the data available. As discussed above, coordinates for rape incidents were masked and listed on the precinct level rather than the exact latitude/longitude coordinates to maintain confidentiality of the data. As a result, geospatial mapping of rape crime across the city appears scattered. In addition, the outliers in the data set may skew the data set, for instance, a murder that occurred in 1982 was only reported in 2015. While these outliers have been taken care of in the report, it may nevertheless affect the accuracy of our research.

Nevertheless, the report has wider implications in drawing attention to the long time gap in reporting rape incidents, particularly in states with statutes of limitations on filing rape charges. 34 states have statutes of limitations, preventing victims who delay reporting the crime from pressing charges. The data presented in this report exemplifies the long ‘culture of silence’ on rape, suggesting that more education and support networks needs to be in place to eradicate social stigma surrounding rape. Our research is also limited by the fact that a significant portion of rape incidents often go unreported, with RAINN estimating that only 32 percent of rapes are reported.

Conclusion

To conclude, the report has presented evidence supporting the ‘culture of silence’ surrounding rape. It has also highlighted key areas in the city where more efforts in education and support can be improved. Overall the trends observed in all felonies can also inform and improve policing in the city.
